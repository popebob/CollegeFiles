FRC's Letter sent to the American Academy of Physicians
printer-friendly page
email this item
Summary:
This letter was sent by FRC to the American Academy of Family Physicians urging them to reject a homosexual activist resolutions that seeks to legitimize homosexual adoption and promote homosexual partner benefits.

October 10, 2002
The American Academy of Family Physicians
11400 Tomahawk Creek Parkway
P. O. Box 11210
Shawnee Mission, Kansas 66207-1210

Dear Members of the Congress of Delegates:

It has come to our attention that the Congress of Delegates of the American Academy of Family Physicians will be asked to adopt resolutions regarding sexual orientation at its meeting in San Diego next week. According to one homosexual advocacy organization, "These resolutions call for:

·  equal rights for gay and lesbian parents;

·  the right of the children of same-sex parents to have the psychological and legal security of a certain legal relationship to both parents through second-parent and joint adoption; and

·  health and life insurance benefits for domestic partners."

We urge you to reject each of these proposals.

Individuals who identify themselves as gay or lesbian already enjoy every right guaranteed by the U.S. Constitution, including the equal protection of the law. When pro-homosexual activists speak of "equal rights," what they really seek is special protection under civil rights statutes comparable to that granted for other characteristics such as race or sex. However, unlike any other characteristics so protected, homosexual behavior is not inborn, involuntary, immutable, innocuous, nor in the Constitution. Homosexuals as a group also do not suffer the economic deprivation or political powerlessness that are sometimes thought to justify special protection. Therefore, "equal rights" legislation is neither necessary nor logically justifiable.

Furthermore, far from being protected, homosexual behavior should be actively discouraged on medical grounds. Research on homosexuals shows that they are more promiscuous than heterosexuals.[1] They also have higher rates of sexually transmitted disease,[2] substance abuse,[3] and mental health problems.[4] In addition, research shows that being in a homosexual "partnership" does little to diminish these risks. Such relationships on average are short-lived,[5] are not characterized by sexual fidelity,[6] include unhealthy sexual acts,[7] and feature higher rates of domestic violence than heterosexual marriages.[8] Parents may have a "right" to smoke tobacco or abuse alcohol as well, but surely these are not "rights" whose exercise should be defended by family physicians. The same is true of homosexual behavior.

As for homosexual parents, the evidence cited above demonstrates clearly that the homosexual lifestyle is inconsistent with the proper raising of children. This fact has been obscured, however, by claims that studies of gay parenting show no harmful effects. However, a careful analysis of 49 studies of homosexual parenting has revealed major problems with that research, including:

·  "Unclear hypotheses and research designs"

·  "Missing or inadequate comparison groups"

·  "Self-constructed, unreliable and invalid measurements;" and

·  "Missing or inadequate statistical analysis"

In fact, researchers Robert Lerner and Althea Nagai "found at least one fatal research flaw in all forty-nine studies. As a result, they conclude that no generalizations can reliably be made based on any of these studies. For these reasons the studies are no basis for good science or good public policy."9

One well-designed study not included in Lerner and Nagai's critique, however, did compare children of heterosexual cohabiting couples, heterosexual married couples, and homosexual couples. It found that children of married couples did the best, and the children of homosexual couples did the worst, in nine of the thirteen categories measured. The author concluded, "Overall, the study has shown that children of married couples are more likely to do well at school, in academic and social terms, than children of cohabiting heterosexual and homosexual couples."10

As for so-called "domestic partnerships," abundant research on family structure proves that children do best when raised by a mother and father who are united in marriage. There is a growing body of evidence showing that children raised in traditional families by a mother and father are happier, healthier, and more successful than children raised in other family structures.11 Even having two homosexual parents is no substitute, as shown by the study cited above.

The risk to the health and well-being of both adults and children as a result of the growing affirmation and subsidization of homosexual behavior should outweigh the narrow self-interest of expanding health insurance coverage to a small number of additional children and "partners."

For the sake of America's children and families, I urge you to reject these proposed resolutions.

Sincerely,

Peter Sprigg
Senior Director of Culture Studies
Family Research Council
Washington, D. C.

END NOTES
